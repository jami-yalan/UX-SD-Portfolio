About

The designer
behind the work

My background is in industrial design, working with physical materials and tangible objects. But somewhere along the way I realised that working in digital products could give me the ability to solve problems with a material that is incredibly flexible and familiar to almost everyone, and allows me to iterate with people's input throughout the process and even after a release. Physical products rarely allow that, and certainly not as fast.

Over time I found myself drawn to the questions behind the screen. Why is this product failing if the interface seems right. In research sessions I kept hearing frustrations that had nothing to do with the screens I was designing, they were about the broader service around them. That shift pulled me into service design: understanding the whole system behind a single touchpoint, every process, every handoff, every moment where something could go wrong for a real person.

I started as a UX designer in tech-first environments, from a main banking app at Interbank to a large SaaS platform like Autodesk Construction Cloud. That gave me a strong technical foundation: Figma, design systems, prototyping, working closely with engineering, understanding platform architecture at a product level. That foundation has not gone away. My vertical is service design and strategic thinking. The horizontal is genuinely there.

01
Academias
Crehana · EdTech · Strategic Design
+
Context

Crehana is Latin America's largest EdTech platform, with over 20,000 courses and a subscription model. An NPS survey showed the most common reason students dropped their subscription was "I can't find what I need." Management interpreted this as a search and taxonomy problem, and my team was tasked with fixing how existing courses were bundled into a product called Especializaciones.

Problem

The company had already tried bundling courses twice before, under different names, and both had underperformed. Rather than accepting the brief at face value, I wanted to understand why the pattern kept repeating before committing to the same direction a third time. My hypothesis was that the problem was not the interface. It was something upstream.

Design Process
  • Qualitative research: Ran 24 interviews across 7 user segments: Creative, Tech, Business, PAYG, Premium, Churn and Detractor. The goal was to understand what users actually meant when they said they could not find what they needed, and whether fixing the bundle interface would address the real problem.
  • Synthesis: Used affinity mapping to cluster findings across all 24 participants. The key insight was that users were not struggling to find courses. The courses they needed often did not exist in the format they needed. Especializaciones failed because it bundled standalone courses that each started with a basic introduction, repeated topics and did not build on each other.
  • Reframing the brief: Presented findings to stakeholders and made the case for redirecting the project scope entirely: from interface redesign to a new product concept built on purpose-made sequenced content.
  • Design criteria workshop: Ran a MoSCoW session to translate research into shared design principles, giving the team a reference point for what the new experience had to do and what it would not replicate from previous attempts.
  • Service architecture: Mapped the full Minimum Viable Service to show what the end-to-end experience needed to be, covering 5 teams and 3 service phases: purchase, learning and project.
Outcomes
Brief reframed from UI fix to new product concept 24 interviews across 7 user segments Stakeholder buy-in secured for redirected scope MoSCoW design criteria established as team reference Full MVS blueprint covering 5 teams and 3 service phases Academias launched as a new product line on the platform
Solution

Academias replaced the flat bundle model with a branching learning structure. Students start with a skill assessment to place their level, then follow a structured path from foundational knowledge to progressive specialisation. At each stage they can choose which direction to go deeper. Courses within an Academy are purpose-built for the sequence, not repurposed standalone content. A user in one interview described exactly this: "like a tree, a base and a trunk and then you choose which branch to go down."

Takeaways

The most important decision on this project was to question the brief before accepting it. Crehana had tried to solve this problem twice before without asking why the previous attempts had not worked. The research answered that question and changed what we built. That shift, from fixing a UI to redesigning a product, came directly from 24 conversations with real users.

02
Design System Migration
Autodesk Construction Cloud · SaaS Platform
+
Context

Autodesk Construction Cloud had been acquiring products to unify them into a single platform. BuildingConnected Pro was one of those products, with its own visual identity, distinct colours, navigation patterns and components that did not exist in Alloy, the shared design system for the entire ACC platform. Embedded in the Autodesk team as a contractor through Globant, my role was to migrate those unique elements into Alloy without breaking the experience for existing users or compromising the system's consistency.

Problem

BCpro had components that had no equivalent in Alloy, and a colour palette with strong contrast that the neutral Alloy palette did not replicate. The challenge was not just applying new styles. It was redesigning specific components so they could be absorbed into the shared library while still feeling right to people who had been using BCpro for years. Changes to one product had downstream effects across the platform, which meant every decision needed to be evaluated against the wider system, not just the single product.

Design Process
  • Audit and sitemap: Audited existing screens and built a sitemap to understand the full scope of what needed to change and identify which flows contained components requiring custom work.
  • System deep-dive: Read through the Alloy documentation and libraries thoroughly before designing anything, to understand what already existed and where the genuine gaps were.
  • Component redesign: Designed new versions of BCpro-specific components including status buttons, cards, modals and colour usage, preserving interaction patterns users were familiar with while aligning them to Alloy guidelines.
  • State documentation: Documented each component with full interaction states (default, hover, pressed, focused) for development handoff.
  • Staged delivery: Delivered changes in stages so the engineering team could plan the workload incrementally rather than facing a single large migration.
Outcomes
Cards, modals, status buttons and colour decisions migrated to Alloy New components contributed back to the shared Alloy library Full interaction state documentation for each component Colour palette updated and optimised for colour blind users Delivered in staged increments to reduce engineering risk Migration rolled out to 100% of BuildingConnected Pro users
Solution

A set of redesigned BCpro components built to fit within the Alloy system without requiring users to relearn the product. Each component we contributed back to Alloy had to work for BCpro and for every other product sharing the library. That constraint shaped every decision: designing not just for BCpro users but for the teams maintaining the platform after we were done.

Takeaways

Working within an established design system means the hardest decisions are rarely about aesthetics. They happen when the system does not have an answer for something your product needs, and you have to decide whether to bend the system or redesign the component. What got us through those moments was testing quickly with real users rather than debating in the abstract.

03
Operations Platform
Farmacias Peruanas · Internal SaaS · Design System
+
Context

Farmacias Peruanas is one of Peru's largest pharmacy chains, serving millions of customers across Lima through a network of physical stores and a growing delivery operation. When COVID-19 hit in March 2020, the existing operations tooling collapsed: staff fell ill, stores had to close unexpectedly, and a fragmented set of tools built on different platforms meant the team could not control delivery capacity in real time. Customers were placing orders that could not be fulfilled.

Problem

The operations team needed to close stores, pause delivery windows and manage driver assignments without relying on manual workarounds across multiple disconnected tools. There were two layers: an immediate emergency need to control capacity during the pandemic, and a longer-term need for a unified platform to replace the fragmented tooling. All of this had to be designed, built and shipped in under a month. I led the design and directed a junior designer through the process.

Design Process
  • Prioritisation workshop: Rapid session with operations and product team to map all existing tools, identify critical pain points and agree on a phased delivery sequence based on emergency impact.
  • Design system first: Rather than designing screens in isolation, started with a design system that could work across web and mobile. Each module shipped with consistent components from day one.
  • Capacity scheduling: Designed the AM/PM, Scheduled and Express delivery window management modules, including state logic for open, closed and partially available windows across multiple store locations.
  • Driver app: Designed a separate Android app for delivery drivers covering order assignment, routing, status updates, cancellation flows and offline error states for low-connectivity areas.
  • Modular shipping: Each module designed and shipped independently in sprints, with the design system ensuring visual and interaction consistency across the growing suite.
Outcomes
Full design system covering mobile and web, shipped in under a month Capacity scheduling module across AM/PM, Scheduled and Express delivery types Android driver app with full order management and offline error handling Store open/close controls deployed across Lima's pharmacy network Operations team of 20 able to manage delivery capacity in real time Platform became foundation for further internal tooling after launch
Solution

A modular operations platform built on a single design system, deployed across web for store managers and Android for delivery drivers. The capacity scheduling tool gave the operations team real-time control over delivery windows across all store locations. The driver app handled the full order lifecycle from assignment to delivery, including cancellation flows, zone reassignment and offline states for drivers in low-connectivity areas. Both products were designed to be learned quickly by people under significant operational stress.

Takeaways

Designing a design system before designing screens felt counterintuitive under that kind of time pressure, but it was the right call. Every subsequent module was faster to design and more consistent because the components already existed. The driver app taught me that designing for high-stress, low-connectivity environments requires ruthless simplicity: every confirmation step, every error state, every empty state matters when someone is working in the field and does not have time to figure out what went wrong.
