About

The designer
behind the work

My background is in industrial design, which taught me early that aesthetics and function are the same question, just asked from different angles. That lesson has stayed with me and informs my current focus on digital product design.

The ability to iterate with people's input is what pulled me fully into digital. Physical products can rarely allow that, and certainly not as fast. Understanding who you are designing for, and keeping that understanding close throughout the process, is something I care about deeply and find genuinely fulfilling.

Part of keeping that understanding close is keeping testing close to the work the whole way through. Running it alongside the whole process is what makes products easy to understand and enjoyable to use, catching assumptions before they ship and grounding decisions in real behaviour rather than best guesses. Whether the navigation holds up, whether the layout works for someone who needs larger text, whether what seemed obvious in Figma turns out not to be when a real person uses it.

And this approach works best when everyone in the room is fluent in the process but brings different backgrounds and ways of thinking, which is why I find real value in working alongside less experienced designers. Fresh eyes catch things that familiarity hides, and teaching the practice and helping others develop their own instincts is something I genuinely enjoy. It tends to make the work better for everyone involved.

01
Frilea
Interbank
+
Context

Peru has a large informal economy and a significant portion of the workforce operates outside traditional employment structures. Freelancers earn variable income and rarely have the payslips or employment contracts that banks require to open accounts or access credit. In 2019, the CEO of Interbank challenged Labentana Innovation Lab and the Digital Channels Team to answer a single question: how might we offer a valuable financial alternative to unbanked people? Even with a hypothesis in mind, they wanted the team to do the research first and find out who the real user was and whether the idea held up.

Problem

Existing banking products were built around salaried employment: fixed monthly income, regular payments, predictable behaviour. Freelancers did not fit that model. Standard onboarding required documents they did not have. The challenge was not to improve an existing product for this segment. It was to find out whether a product for them made sense at all, and if so, what it should be.

Design Process
  • Discovery: Working alongside business designers and researchers, I led the service design work across discovery and concept validation. Desk research, expert interviews and field interviews with independently employed people pointed us toward a specific segment: freelancers aged 20–40, with internet access and variable income, who distrusted formal financial institutions. We called them los inconformes — pragmatic, cautious about debt, and allergic to paperwork.
  • Three pain points identified: No access to employment benefits like healthcare or compensation; payment and tax complexity keeping them unbanked — the system felt impossible to navigate alone, more than unwillingness; and no guaranteed pipeline of work, the pain point that came up most consistently across interviews.
  • Three concepts: Each designed to address one pain point directly. Tribu: a community platform where freelancers could organise collectively to negotiate group health insurance and other benefits. Quadre: an income management tool that auto-calculated tax obligations from incoming payments. Freelea: matched freelancers with relevant gigs while acting as a financial intermediary, building their credit score in parallel.
Outcomes
Three service concepts developed and tested via live social media experiment 400+ sign-ups in four days validated genuine demand before any build Target segment redefined from individual freelancers to small independent businesses Concept seeded into Labentana incubator and launched as a live product within Interbank's suite
Solution

Before committing to any build, we ran a social media experiment to test which concept generated the most genuine demand. Freelea got a 1.9% CTR and more than 400 sign-ups in four days, with no product built yet. That data became the business case for taking the concept into Labentana's incubator. The Wizard of Oz phase that followed sharpened the direction further: by simulating the service manually with real users, we found that the strongest fit was not the individual freelancer we had originally designed for, but small independent businesses looking for talent. The validated concept was later renamed Frilea and developed into a live product by a subsequent team.

Both of those came before a single screen was designed, and both of them shaped everything that followed.

02
Declaration of Health
Royal London
+
Context

Royal London is the UK's largest mutual life insurance company. Protection customers manage their policies through independent financial advisers, which means Royal London often has limited direct contact with end customers. This project sat within the protection servicing team and focused on the communications customers receive when their policy is at risk of lapsing.

Problem

When a customer misses payments and their policy lapses, Royal London sends a Declaration of Health form to reinstate it. The problem came to light through a complaint: a customer had just been diagnosed with cancer. In the stress of that, she missed an automatic payment, her policy lapsed, and when she tried to reinstate it she completed the declaration honestly. Because of her diagnosis, we could not take her on again. The process had worked exactly as designed. But it had failed her completely. The real issue lived earlier in the journey, in how we communicated before customers ever reached that form.

Design Process
  • Research and iteration: Ran 5 rounds of qualitative testing with 24 participants total through UserTesting, using the actual letters as artefacts to anchor conversations. Participants included customers who had experienced financial hardship, serious illness or bereavement.
  • Synthesis: Each round revealed where the tone was missing, where the information was unclear, and where customers felt abandoned rather than supported. The key reframe was that the problem was not the Declaration of Health form itself but how little support existed before customers ever got there.
  • Stakeholder alignment: Facilitated feedback sessions with marketing, subject matter experts and vulnerable customer panels to pressure-test tone, clarity and accessibility across the full journey.
  • Communication redesign: Redesigned the full arc of lapse communications, adding a new reminder stage and integrating Helping Hand support resources and simplified payment update instructions throughout.
Outcomes
3-stage communication journey designed to replace single notice 24 vulnerable customers involved in iterative qualitative testing across 5 rounds Helping Hand resources integrated across all lapse communications Clear payment update instructions added to reduce friction for customers in distress Designs handed over to implementation team at project close
Solution

Redesigned the full communication journey for lapsing policies. Added a new reminder letter 20 days after the first notice, creating a 3-stage arc: warning at 35 days, reminder at 20 days, confirmation of cancellation. All three letters now include signposting to Helping Hand, Royal London's free wellbeing service. QR codes were added for easy mobile access. Instructions for updating payment methods were simplified and made prominent throughout.

Takeaways

The most valuable thing I did on this project was to reframe the challenge from a form redesign problem to a communication project. We found quickly that the gap was in how we communicated before the form was ever needed. This reframe came directly from listening to customers, and it changed the entire direction of the work.

03
Operations Platform
Farmacias Peruanas
+
Context

Farmacias Peruanas is one of Peru's largest pharmacy chains, serving millions of customers across Lima through a network of physical stores and a growing delivery operation. When the COVID-19 pandemic hit in March 2020, the existing operations tooling collapsed: staff fell ill, stores had to close unexpectedly, and a fragmented set of tools built on different platforms meant the team could not control delivery capacity in real time. Customers were placing orders that could not be fulfilled, which was causing widespread dissatisfaction and operational chaos.

Problem

The operations team needed to be able to close stores, pause delivery windows and manage driver assignments without relying on manual workarounds across multiple disconnected tools. The problem had two layers: an immediate emergency need to control capacity during the pandemic, and a longer-term need for a unified platform to replace the fragmented tooling entirely. All of this had to be designed, built and shipped in under a month.

Design Process
  • Prioritisation workshop: Ran a rapid workshop with the operations and product team to map all existing tools, identify critical pain points and agree on a phased delivery sequence based on emergency impact.
  • Design system first: Rather than designing screens in isolation, started with a design system that could work across web and mobile. This meant each module shipped with consistent components from day one.
  • Capacity scheduling: Designed the AM/PM, Scheduled and Express delivery window management modules, including state logic for open, closed, and partially available windows across multiple store locations.
  • Driver app: Designed a separate Android app for delivery drivers covering order assignment, routing, status updates, cancellation flows and offline error states for areas with poor connectivity.
  • Modular shipping: Each module was designed and shipped independently in sprints, with the design system ensuring visual and interaction consistency across the growing suite.
Outcomes
Full design system covering mobile and web, shipped in under a month Capacity scheduling module across AM/PM, Scheduled and Express delivery types Android driver app with full order management and offline error handling Store open/close controls deployed across Lima's pharmacy network Operations team of 20 able to manage delivery capacity in real time during the pandemic Platform became the foundation for further internal tooling after the initial launch
Solution

A modular operations platform built on a single design system, deployed across web for store managers and Android for delivery drivers. The capacity scheduling tool gave the operations team real-time control over delivery windows across all store locations, with clear state logic and confirmation flows to prevent errors under pressure. The driver app handled the full order lifecycle from assignment to delivery, including cancellation flows, zone reassignment and offline states for drivers in low-connectivity areas. Both products were designed to be learned quickly by people under significant operational stress.

Takeaways

Designing a design system before designing screens felt counterintuitive under that kind of time pressure, but it was the right call. Every subsequent module was faster to design and more consistent because the components already existed. The driver app taught me that designing for high-stress, low-connectivity environments requires ruthless simplicity: every confirmation step, every error state, every empty state matters when someone is working in the field and does not have time to figure out what went wrong.

I enjoy using AI tools for creative coding and bringing ideas to life quickly. One of the projects I am most proud of is my wedding website. I wanted it to feel genuinely personal rather than generic, and I did not want to rely on external services for livestreaming, gift management or photo sharing. It also needed to be practically useful: almost all of my guests were travelling from Peru to Scotland, which meant transport, weather, accommodation and logistics all needed to be clear.

Built from scratch using AI, HTML, CSS and JavaScript, deployed on GitHub Pages with a Google Sheets backend for form responses. No templates, no shortcuts. Bilingual, responsive, with custom animations and live forms.
